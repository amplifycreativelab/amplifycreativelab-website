<h2>Why Commission Costs Are Eating Your Profits</h2>

<p>
  Third‑party delivery platforms have become a lifeline for many Perth restaurants, but they come at a steep price. UberEats, Menulog, and Deliveroo typically take 25–35% of each order as commission, plus additional service fees. On a $40 order, that’s $10–$14 gone before you’ve covered food costs, labour, and overhead.
</p>

<p>
  The alternative—direct ordering through your own website—lets you keep the full revenue. Yet most venues struggle to move customers away from the convenience of UberEats. The key to shifting that behaviour isn’t just a functional ordering system; it’s <strong>visual persuasion</strong>. Customers choose what looks best, and if your UberEats thumbnail is more appealing than your website photo, you’re handing over 30% of your revenue unnecessarily.
</p>

<h2>The Visual Gap: Why UberEats Often Wins</h2>

<p>
  UberEats invests heavily in optimising its platform for visual appeal. It enforces consistent image dimensions, encourages high‑quality thumbnails, and even offers free photography to some partners. As a result, your dishes can look more enticing on UberEats than on your own site—especially if your website photos are outdated, poorly lit, or shot on a smartphone.
</p>

<p>
  This creates a perverse incentive: customers see a beautiful photo on UberEats, tap to order, and you pay a hefty commission. If your own website presented the same dish with equal or better visual impact, many of those customers would happily order directly.
</p>

</p>

<ul>

  <li><strong>Boost average order value:</strong> Appealing photos of high‑margin items (desserts, drinks, sides) encourage add‑ons.</li>
  <li><strong>Improve brand perception:</strong> Consistent, professional visuals signal that your venue is credible and cares about the customer experience.</li>
  <li><strong>Enhance SEO:</strong> Optimised images with descriptive alt text help your website rank higher in Google searches for “Perth restaurant delivery” or “order [cuisine] online”.</li>

<p>

<h2>Optimising Your Photography for Direct Ordering</h2>

<p>
  To make your direct ordering system compete with UberEats, your photos need to meet—and exceed—platform standards. Follow these guidelines:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Shoot for thumbnails:</strong> Ensure dishes are recognisable and appetising even at small sizes (UberEats uses 800×800px; your website should use similar dimensions for ordering grids).</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain consistency:</strong> Use the same styling, lighting, and editing across all images so customers experience a seamless brand journey from platform to website.</li>
  <li><strong>Highlight your unique selling points:</strong> If you use local ingredients, have a famous chef, or offer sustainable packaging, show it in the photos.</li>
  <li><strong>Include “order direct” cues:</strong> In lifestyle shots, subtly incorporate elements that point to your own brand (e.g., your logo on packaging, a tablet showing your website).</li>
</ul>

</p>

<h2>Technical Setup: Making Direct Ordering Frictionless</h2>

<p>
  Even the best photography won’t help if your ordering process is clunky. Your direct ordering system must be as smooth as UberEats. Key elements include:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Mobile‑first design:</strong> Over 70% of delivery orders are placed on phones. Your ordering page must load quickly, display images clearly, and have a simple checkout.</li>
  <li><strong>Integrated payment gateway:</strong> Use a trusted provider like Stripe or Square for secure, one‑click payments.</li>
  <li><strong>Real‑time availability:</strong> If you’re using a third‑party delivery service (like DoorDash Drive or Uber Direct), ensure your website reflects accurate delivery times and fees.</li>
  <li><strong>Prominent photography:</strong> Place your best images front and centre, with zoom functionality so customers can see details.</li>
</ul>
