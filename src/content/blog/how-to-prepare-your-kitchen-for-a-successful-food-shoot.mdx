  Preparing your kitchen for a food photography shoot involves cleaning, organizing, gathering props, planning lighting, and coordinating with your photographer. <p>
  Whether you're shooting for a new menu, updating your website, or creating content for social media, the effort you put into preparation directly impacts the quality of your final images. In Perth's competitive hospitality scene, where visuals often decide a customer's first impression, getting this right is crucial.
</p>

<h2>Step 1 – Clean and Declutter Your Workspace</h2>
<p>
  A clean kitchen isn't just about hygiene—it's about creating a neutral canvas that lets your food be the star. Before the photographer arrives, take time to:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Clear countertops</strong> of all non‑essential equipment, utensils, and personal items.</li>
  <li><strong>Wipe down surfaces</strong> to remove grease, water spots, and fingerprints that can show up in reflections.</li>
  <li><strong>Hide trash bins, cleaning supplies, and storage containers</strong> out of frame.</li>
  <li><strong>Check the floor</strong> for spills or debris that could appear in wide‑angle shots.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  Remember: the camera sees everything. A cluttered background distracts from your dishes and can make your kitchen look unprofessional. For Perth venues, where natural light often highlights dust and smudges, a thorough clean is especially important.
</p>

<h2>Step 2 – Gather Your Props and Styling Tools</h2>
<p>
  Professional food photography relies on props to add context and visual interest. Gather these items ahead of time to avoid last‑minute scrambling:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Plates, bowls, and servingware</strong> in various sizes and styles that match your brand.</li>
  <li><strong>Cutlery, napkins, and glassware</strong> that are spotless and free of watermarks.</li>
  <li><strong>Textured linens, wooden boards, or stone slabs</strong> to create different backgrounds.</li>
  <li><strong>Fresh herbs, citrus wedges, spices, and other garnishes</strong> that can be added during the shoot.</li>
  <li><strong>Small tools</strong> like tweezers, brushes, and spray bottles for precise styling.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  If you're unsure which props suit your venue's style, look at Perth food photography trends: coastal cafés often use light, natural materials, while city bars might lean toward dark, industrial textures. Your photographer can also advise on what works best for your dishes. For more styling ideas, see our <a href="/blog/food-photography-tips-restaurants">food photography tips for restaurants</a>.
</p>

<h2>Step 3 – Plan Your Lighting Setup</h2>
<p>
  Lighting is the single most important factor in food photography. Perth's abundant natural light is a huge advantage, but you need to plan how to use it:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Identify the best window</strong> in your kitchen—ideally one that provides soft, indirect light for most of the day.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid direct sunlight</strong>, which creates harsh shadows and blown‑out highlights. Use sheer curtains or diffusers if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Have backup lighting ready</strong> for evening shoots or cloudy days. Continuous LED panels or softboxes can mimic natural light.</li>
  <li><strong>Turn off overhead fluorescent lights</strong>, which cast unflattering yellow or green tones on food.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  Discuss lighting with your photographer beforehand. They may bring additional equipment, but knowing your kitchen's light conditions helps them plan the shoot efficiently.
</p>

<h2>Step 4 – Organize Your Dishes and Ingredients</h2>
<p>
  A well‑organized shoot flows smoothly and keeps food looking fresh. Create a shot list and sequence dishes logically:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Group similar dishes</strong> (e.g., all pasta, all seafood) to reuse styling and lighting setups.</li>
  <li><strong>Start with cold or room‑temperature dishes</strong> that won't wilt or melt under lights.</li>
  <li><strong>Prepare ingredients in stages</strong> so everything is at peak freshness when it's time to shoot.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep a "hero" version of each dish</strong> reserved solely for photography—untouched by tasting or serving.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  For Perth venues, consider seasonal produce availability. Shooting in summer? Highlight local stone fruit, seafood, and salads. In winter, focus on hearty stews, roasted vegetables, and warm desserts. Seasonal ingredients not only look fresh—they also resonate with local customers.
</p>

<h2>Step 5 – Coordinate with Your Photographer</h2>
<p>
  Clear communication with your photographer ensures everyone is on the same page. Share these details before the shoot:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Your shot list</strong> and any must‑have images (e.g., signature dishes, new menu items).</li>
  <li><strong>Your brand style guide</strong> or examples of images you like (and don't like).</li>
  <li><strong>Access and parking information</strong> for your venue, especially in busy Perth suburbs.</li>
  <li><strong>Contact numbers</strong> for the day, plus any kitchen safety or protocol reminders.</li>
</ul>
