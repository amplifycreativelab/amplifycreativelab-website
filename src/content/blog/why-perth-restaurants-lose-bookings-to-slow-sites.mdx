<h3>Unoptimized Images (The Visual Paradox)</h3>

<p>
  High‑quality food photography is essential for making dishes look appetizing, but unoptimized images are the #1 cause of slow load times. A single full‑resolution photo shot on a DSLR can be 10–20 MB—enough to stall a mobile page for 10 seconds or more on a typical 4G connection.
</p>

<p>
  <strong>The fix:</strong> Professional image optimization that balances visual quality with file size. We shoot, edit, and export images specifically for the web, using modern formats like WebP, responsive sizing, and CDN delivery to ensure they look stunning <em>and</em> load in a fraction of a second. For more, see our guide on <a href="/blog/how-image-compression-impacts-seo-why-photographers-get-it-wrong">how image compression impacts SEO</a>.
</p>

<h3>Bloated Themes & Outdated Platforms</h3>

<p>
  Many Perth venues still rely on generic WordPress themes or DIY website builders that load hundreds of unnecessary scripts, stylesheets, and plugins. Each extra resource adds precious milliseconds to your load time—and multiplies the risk of compatibility issues, security vulnerabilities, and layout shifts.
</p>

<p>
  <strong>The fix:</strong> Building with modern, lightweight frameworks like Astro that deliver only the essential HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Static sites eliminate database calls and server‑side processing, resulting in near‑instant page loads even on mobile networks. For a comparison, see <a href="/blog/astro-vs-wordpress-hospitality-websites-perth">Astro vs WordPress for hospitality websites</a>.

<h3>Missing Core Web Vitals Optimization</h3>

<p>
  Google's Core Web Vitals (LCP, CLS, INP) measure real‑user experience. If your site scores poorly, you're not only frustrating visitors—you're also being penalised in search rankings, making it harder for new customers to find you.
</p>

<h3>Core Web Vitals Explained</h3>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Metric</th>
      <th>What It Measures</th>
      <th>Target</th>
      <th>Impact on Diners</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>LCP</strong></td>
      <td>Time for main content to appear</td>
      <td>Under 2.5s</td>
      <td>If slow, customers leave before seeing menu</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>CLS</strong></td>
      <td>Visual stability (elements jumping)</td>
      <td>Under 0.1</td>
      <td>If high, customers tap wrong button</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>INP</strong></td>
      <td>Response time to interactions</td>
      <td>Under 200ms</td>
      <td>If slow, booking form feels broken</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Largest Contentful Paint (LCP):</strong> How long it takes for the main content (e.g., your hero image or menu headline) to appear. Aim for under 2.5 seconds.</li>
  <li><strong>Cumulative Layout Shift (CLS):</strong> Measures visual stability—elements jumping around while the page loads. A high CLS can cause customers to tap the wrong button or lose their place in your menu.</li>
  <li><strong>Interaction to Next Paint (INP):</strong> Tracks how quickly your site responds to clicks, taps, or keyboard input. Slow interaction frustrates users trying to navigate your menu or fill out a booking form.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions: Website Speed & Bookings</h2>

<aside className="faq-section">
  <h3>How do I know if my restaurant website is slow?</h3>
  <p>
    Run your site through <strong>Google PageSpeed Insights</strong> (free). If your Lighthouse Performance score is below 70, or any Core Web Vital shows red/orange, you have problems. Also test on your phone using mobile data (not WiFi) to experience what customers actually see.
  </p>

  <h3>What's an acceptable load time for a restaurant website?</h3>
  <p>
    Under <strong>3 seconds</strong> is acceptable; under <strong>2 seconds</strong> is optimal. Every second beyond 3 dramatically increases bounce rate. For Perth's mobile-first audience, aim for the fastest possible experience.
  </p>

  <h3>How much can speed improvement actually increase bookings?</h3>
  <p>
    Our case studies show <strong>15–40% booking increases</strong> from speed optimization alone. The improvement comes from lower bounce rates (people stay to see your menu), better conversion (faster booking forms), and improved Google rankings (more visibility).
  </p>

  <h3>Is my slow site actually hurting my Google ranking?</h3>
  <p>
    <strong>Yes.</strong> Core Web Vitals are confirmed Google ranking factors. A slow site gets penalised in search results, meaning fewer people find you in the first place. Fix speed and you'll likely see organic traffic improvements within 2–4 weeks.
  </p>

  <h3>Can I just add caching to my existing slow site?</h3>
  <p>
    Caching helps, but it's <strong>rarely enough by itself</strong>. If your images are unoptimized, your theme is bloated, or your hosting is slow, caching alone won't solve the problem. Most slow sites need multiple fixes working together.
  </p>

  <h3>Do I need to rebuild my entire website to make it faster?</h3>
  <p>
    <strong>Not always.</strong> Some sites can be optimized in place (image compression, plugin removal, hosting upgrade). Others with fundamental issues (heavy themes, outdated platforms) benefit more from a rebuild. We assess each case individually during our free audit.
  </p>

  <h3>How long does speed optimization take?</h3>
  <p>
    Optimization of an existing site typically takes <strong>1–2 weeks</strong>. A full performance-focused rebuild takes <strong>4–6 weeks</strong>. The timeline depends on your site's current state and the depth of changes needed.
  </p>
</aside>
