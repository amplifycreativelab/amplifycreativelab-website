<h2>Photography Techniques That Drive Sales</h2>
<p>
  Not all food photography is created equal. To support your menu‚Äëengineering goals, your images need to be strategically crafted.

<h3>1. Lighting That Creates Appetite Appeal</h3>
<p>
  Soft, directional light makes food look fresh, vibrant and irresistible. Harsh kitchen lighting flattens textures and kills appetite. We use natural light or professional softboxes to give each dish depth and warmth, highlighting the details that make customers want to order. Perth's abundant natural light is an advantage here‚Äîbut it must be controlled and diffused for consistency.

<h3>2. Composition That Tells a Story</h3>
<p>
  A well‚Äëcomposed photo doesn't just show a dish‚Äîit tells a story about quality, care and flavour. We frame shots to include subtle context (a linen napkin, a glass of wine, a sprinkle of fresh herbs) that elevates the perceived value of the dish.
</p>

<h3>3. Styling That Highlights Profitability</h3>
<p>
  For high‚Äëmargin items, we add extra visual cues: a drizzle of sauce, a garnish of micro‚Äëherbs, a side of artisan bread. These small touches make the dish feel more premium, justifying a higher price point and increasing its perceived value.
</p>

<h3>4. Consistency Across the Menu</h3>
<p>
  A cohesive visual style across all your menu images builds trust and makes the dining experience feel considered and professional. Inconsistent lighting, angles or styling can confuse customers and dilute your brand.
</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions: Menu Engineering & Photography</h2>

<aside className="faq-section">
  <h3>What is menu engineering?</h3>
  <p>
    Menu engineering is a data‚Äëdriven method of designing your menu to maximise profitability by categorising dishes based on their popularity and profit margin, then using strategic placement, pricing and visual cues (especially photography) to steer customers toward high‚Äëmargin items.
  </p>

  <h3>How does photography affect menu engineering?</h3>
  <p>
    Professional photography acts as a <strong>visual anchor</strong>, drawing customer attention to the dishes you want to sell most. High‚Äëquality images increase order rates for those dishes by 30% or more, making them an essential tool for shifting items from low‚Äëprofit to high‚Äëprofit categories.
  </p>

  <h3>How many dishes should I photograph for menu engineering?</h3>
  <p>
    Start with your <strong>top Stars and Puzzles</strong>‚Äîtypically 8‚Äì12 dishes. These are the items that will deliver the biggest return on your photography investment. Focus on high-margin items first, then expand to the rest of your menu over time.
  </p>

  <h3>Can menu engineering work for small caf√©s?</h3>
  <p>
    <strong>Absolutely.</strong> Menu engineering principles scale to any size venue. Even a small Mount Lawley or Leederville caf√© can identify its most profitable items and use photography to promote them‚Äîoften with dramatic results in average order value.
  </p>

  <h3>How do I calculate which dishes are Stars, Puzzles, etc.?</h3>
  <p>
    Calculate two things per dish: (1) <strong>Food cost percentage</strong> (cost of ingredients / menu price) and (2) <strong>Sales volume</strong> (orders per week/month). Plot dishes on a grid: high margin + high popularity = Star, high margin + low popularity = Puzzle, low margin + high popularity = Plowhorse, low margin + low popularity = Dog.
  </p>

  <h3>Should I photograph every menu item?</h3>
  <p>
    <strong>No‚Äîstrategically selective is often better.</strong> Too many photos can dilute impact. Start with your highest-ROI items (Puzzles and Stars) and key signature dishes. If every dish has a photo, nothing stands out. Use photography to guide attention, not flood it.
  </p>

  <h3>How quickly will I see results from menu engineering changes?</h3>
  <p>
    Most Perth restaurants see measurable order mix changes within <strong>2‚Äì4 weeks</strong> of updating their menu with new photography. Full impact (on average order value and overall profitability) typically emerges over 1‚Äì2 months as staff become familiar with upselling and customers respond to visual cues.
