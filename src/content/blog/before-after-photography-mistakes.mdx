<h2>Why Your Current Photos Are Driving Customers Away</h2>

<p>
  Most Perth restaurants and cafés rely on smartphone photos taken by staff during busy service hours. While convenient, these images suffer from consistent technical flaws that undermine your marketing efforts:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Poor lighting:</strong> Harsh overhead kitchen lights or dim, yellow‑tinted ambient light flatten your dishes and create unappetising shadows.</li>
  <li><strong>Bad composition:</strong> Cropped awkwardly, shot from unflattering angles, or cluttered with distracting background elements.</li>
  <li><strong>Inconsistent styling:</strong> No cohesive visual story—each dish looks like it belongs to a different venue.</li>
  <li><strong>Low resolution and compression:</strong> Blurry, pixelated images that look amateurish on high‑resolution screens.</li>
  <li><strong>Ignoring mobile optimisation:</strong> Images that are too large, slow to load, or display incorrectly on smartphones where most customers browse.</li>
</ul>

<p>
  These mistakes aren’t just aesthetic—they send subconscious signals about your venue’s quality and attention to detail. A customer scrolling through UberEats or Google Maps will instinctively skip a restaurant whose photos look unprofessional, assuming the food matches the visuals.
</p>

<h2>How to Fix These Mistakes Without a Full Shoot</h2>

<p>
  If a professional shoot isn’t in your immediate budget, you can still make significant improvements with these quick fixes:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Lighting hack:</strong> Shoot near a large window during daylight hours, using a white reflector (even a piece of cardboard) to fill shadows.</li>
  <li><strong>Composition rule:</strong> Use the “rule of thirds”—place the dish off‑centre for a more dynamic shot.</li>
  <li><strong>Styling consistency:</strong> Choose a simple, neutral background (like a wooden board or plain tablecloth) and use it for every dish.</li>
  <li><strong>Basic editing:</strong> Use free tools like Canva or Snapseed to adjust brightness, contrast, and colour balance—but avoid heavy filters that distort reality.</li>
  <li><strong>Mobile check:</strong> Test your images on a smartphone before publishing. Ensure they load quickly and look sharp on small screens.</li>
</ul>
