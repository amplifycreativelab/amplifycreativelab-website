<p>
  The "decoy dish" strategy is a menu‑engineering technique that uses a strategically priced item to make your target high‑margin dish appear more attractive. By placing a slightly less appealing but similarly priced option next to your star dish, you steer customers toward the choice that maximises your profit. Combined with professional food photography that highlights the visual superiority of your target dish, this psychological lever can increase sales of high‑margin items by 30–40% for Perth restaurants, cafés and bars. In this article, we explain how the decoy effect works, why photography is its essential partner, and how you can apply both to boost your average order value.
</p>

<h2>The Psychology Behind the Decoy Effect</h2>
<p>
  The decoy effect (or asymmetric dominance) is a cognitive bias where consumers change their preference between two options when a third, less attractive option is introduced. In a restaurant context, this means adding a dish that is priced similarly to your target high‑margin item but offers inferior value—whether through smaller portion size, fewer premium ingredients, or less appealing presentation.
</p>
<p>
  When customers compare the decoy to your target dish, the target appears significantly better value, making it the obvious choice. Research in behavioural economics shows that decoys can shift customer preferences by <strong>up to 50%</strong> toward the intended option. For Perth venues, this isn't just theory—it's a proven sales tool that works because it aligns with how people naturally make decisions.
</p>

<h2>How Photography Amplifies the Decoy Effect</h2>
<p>
  A decoy only works if customers perceive the difference in value. That's where professional food photography becomes your secret weapon. A stunning, professionally lit image of your target dish next to a modestly styled decoy creates a visual contrast that makes the superiority of the target unmistakable.
</p>
<p>
  Consider these photographic techniques that maximise the decoy effect:
</p>

<h3>1. Lighting That Separates Heroes from Decoys</h3>
<p>
  Use soft, directional light on your target dish to make it look vibrant and appetising, while lighting the decoy with flatter, less dramatic illumination. This subtle difference subconsciously signals which dish is the premium choice.
</p>

<h3>2. Composition That Guides the Eye</h3>
<p>
  Place your target dish in the foreground with a shallow depth of field, letting the decoy recede slightly out of focus. This compositional hierarchy tells customers where to look—and what to order.
</p>

<h3>3. Styling That Highlights Value</h3>
<p>
  Add visual cues to your target dish: a drizzle of sauce, a fresh herb garnish, an artisanal side. Leave the decoy plainer. These styling choices reinforce the perceived gap in quality and justify the price difference.
</p>

</p>

<h2>Implementing the Decoy Dish Strategy in Your Perth Venue</h2>
<p>
  Ready to put the decoy effect to work? Follow this four‑step process:
</p>

<h3>Step 1: Identify Your High‑Margin “Star” Dishes</h3>
<p>
  Use your sales data to find dishes with the highest profit margins. These are your targets. If you haven't yet analysed your menu profitability, our article on <a href="/blog/menu-engineering-how-photography-boosts-dish-sales">menu engineering for Perth venues</a> walks you through the process.
</p>

<h3>Step 2: Design a Strategic Decoy</h3>
<p>
  Create a dish that is similar in price to your target but offers noticeably less value—perhaps a smaller portion, a simpler preparation, or fewer premium ingredients. The decoy should be plausible (customers might still order it) but clearly inferior when compared side‑by‑side.
</p>

<h3>Step 3: Photograph the Pair with Intent</h3>
<p>
  Shoot the target and decoy together in the same frame, using the lighting, composition and styling techniques described above. The visual contrast should be obvious even at a glance. If you need professional help, our <a href="/perth-food-photography">Perth food photography services</a> specialise in this kind of strategic imagery.
</p>

<h3>Step 4: Place the Pair Strategically on Your Menu</h3>
<p>
  Position the target and decoy next to each other on your menu, with the target listed first. Use the professional photo of the target dish as a visual anchor, and consider giving the decoy a smaller image or no image at all. This placement reinforces the hierarchy you've created.
</p>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>What is a decoy dish?</h3>
<p>
  A decoy dish is a strategically designed menu item that is priced similarly to a target high‑margin dish but offers inferior value. Its purpose is to make the target dish appear more attractive, steering customers toward the option that delivers higher profit for your venue.
</p>

<h3>Does the decoy dish need to be photographed?</h3>
<p>
  Yes—photographing the decoy alongside the target dish creates a visual contrast that reinforces the difference in value. Professional photography ensures the contrast is clear and appetising, maximising the psychological impact of the decoy effect.
</p>

<h3>How many decoy dishes should I use?</h3>
<p>
  Start with one or two high‑margin categories (e.g., mains, drinks). Introduce a decoy for each target dish you want to promote. Too many decoys can confuse customers, so focus on your most profitable items first.
</p>

<h3>Can decoy strategies work for small cafés?</h3>
<p>
  Absolutely. The decoy effect scales to any size venue. Even a small café can use a decoy to boost sales of its highest‑margin coffee blend, pastry or lunch special. The key is careful pricing and visual presentation.
</p>
