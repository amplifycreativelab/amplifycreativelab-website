<h2>The SEO Problem with PDFs</h2>
<p>
  Google treats PDF menus like black boxes. While Google <em>can</em> index
  PDFs, it cannot effectively "read" individual dishes inside an image-based
  PDF. That means when someone searches for "gluten free pizza Perth" or "vegan
  lasagna Northbridge," your PDF menu won't help you appear in those results.
</p>

<p>
  PDF clicks often behave like a "dead end" in analytics. A user clicks the PDF,
  downloads it, then either stays on the page or bounces—neither action sends
  positive engagement signals to Google. Worse, PDFs rarely support structured
  data like proper <code>Menu</code> schema, which means you're missing out on
  rich search result features that could make your restaurant stand out.
</p>

<p>
  For <strong>Perth restaurant SEO</strong>, this is particularly damaging.
  Local SEO relies on Google understanding exactly what you offer, where you
  offer it, and how customers can access it. PDF menus create barriers at every
  step.
</p>

<h2>The User Experience (UX) Nightmare</h2>
<p>
  Here's the reality: Over 80% of restaurant searches happen on mobile devices.
  Tourists browsing Fremantle, locals searching for lunch in the CBD, or
  families planning dinner in Subiaco—all are using their phones to find places
  to eat.
</p>

<p>PDF menus create multiple UX problems:</p>

<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Download time:</strong> PDFs require downloading data, which is slow
    on mobile networks and eats into customers' data caps.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Poor accessibility:</strong> Small text that requires pinch-zooming
    is difficult to read, especially in bright daylight or while walking.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>No mobile optimization:</strong> PDFs don't adapt to screen size,
    creating a frustrating experience on any device smaller than a desktop.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Lost opportunities:</strong> You can't add "Order Now" buttons,
    highlight chef's specials, or integrate directly with booking systems.
  </li>
</ul>

<p>
  The fundamental problem is simple:
  <strong>You're making hungry people work too hard to give you money.</strong>
  In Perth's competitive hospitality market, that's a luxury you can't afford.
</p>

<h2>The Solution — HTML/Responsive Menus</h2>
<p>
  A modern, <strong>mobile-first restaurant website</strong> builds menus
  directly into your site's code, organized by clear sections (Starters, Mains,
  Desserts, Drinks). This approach solves every problem that PDFs create:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Lightning fast:</strong> No downloads, just instant loading of text
    that's optimized for mobile networks.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>SEO-friendly:</strong> Every dish name, description, and price is
    searchable by Google, helping you rank for specific menu items.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Easy updates:</strong> Change prices, add seasonal items, or remove
    sold-out dishes in minutes without uploading new files.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Better accessibility:</strong> Large, readable text, proper
    contrast, and semantic HTML that works with screen readers.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Conversion features:</strong> Add photos, highlight specials, and
    integrate with booking or ordering systems directly.
  </li>
</ul>

<p>
  For <strong>Perth cafés and restaurants</strong>, this means customers can
  quickly find exactly what they're looking for—whether it's "vegetarian
  options," "kids menu," or specific dietary requirements—and make decisions
  faster.
</p>
